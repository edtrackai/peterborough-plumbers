### 2.3 Legal & Safety Boundaries

**Gas Work:**
Under the Gas Safety (Installation and Use) Regulations 1998, all gas work in England must be carried out by a Gas Safe registered engineer. HSE guidance states that a non-registered person may carry out "wet work" (water pipes, radiators), but any work on the gas boiler itself and the final connection of water pipework to the boiler must be done by a Gas Safe registered engineer.

**Landlord Responsibilities:**
Annual gas safety checks must be carried out by a Gas Safe registered engineer. Records must be kept for 2 years. Existing tenants must receive a copy within 28 days. New tenants must receive a copy before move-in. These responsibilities cannot be transferred to the tenant.

**Unvented Hot-Water Systems:**
Approved Document G requires user warnings stating "Do not remove or adjust any component part of this unvented water heater." If hot water flows from the discharge pipe, the heater must be switched off and the installer contacted. Discharge from safety devices must go to a safe location where it does not endanger people.

**Water Regulations / Notification:**
WaterSafe guidance states that certain planned plumbing work must be notified to the local water supplier before work starts — including baths over 230 litres, bidets with upward spray or flexible hose, pumps or boosters over 12 litres per minute, some irrigation systems, and large pools or ponds.

**Drainage:**
Approved Document H covers foul-water drainage above and below ground, pipe sizes, pipe protection, manholes and inspection chambers. It explicitly states that laying and maintaining drains are hazardous operations.

**Electrical Work:**
Approved Document P governs electrical safety in dwellings. When plumbing/heating and electrics overlap (boiler breaker tripping, controls not powering, electric shower faults), the bot must route correctly — either to the company's engineer if qualified, or advise the customer that an electrician may be needed.

### 7.1 GENERAL PLUMBING REPAIRS

**Scope:**
Dripping taps, leaking tap bases, loose taps, stop-tap issues, isolation-valve issues, leaking pipe joints, trap leaks, visible pipe leaks, overflows, minor plumbing faults, visible fitting failures.

**How a Professional Plumber Repairs These (On-Site Process):**

1. **Arrival & Assessment** — Engineer arrives, confirms the reported fault, visually inspects the affected area, and checks for any secondary damage (water staining, damp, swelling).
2. **Isolation** — Turns off water supply locally (isolation valve or service valve) or at the mains stop tap if no local isolation exists. Confirms water is off before proceeding.
3. **Disassembly** — Removes the faulty component (e.g., tap headgear, cartridge, compression fitting, trap). Notes the make/model/size for replacement.
4. **Diagnosis** — Identifies the root cause — worn washer, failed ceramic cartridge, corroded olive, cracked trap body, degraded O-ring, overtightened compression joint, or lime scale build-up.
5. **Repair or Replacement:**
   - *Washers/O-rings:* Replaced with correct size and type. Re-seated and tightened to spec.
   - *Ceramic cartridges:* Old cartridge removed, new one fitted and tested for both hot and cold operation.
   - *Compression joints:* Olive and fitting inspected. If the olive is scored or the pipe end damaged, the joint is re-made with a new olive and fresh PTFE or jointing compound.
   - *Push-fit joints:* Checked for correct insertion depth and pipe support. Re-made if the grab ring or O-ring has failed.
   - *Traps:* Old trap removed, new trap fitted (bottle trap, P-trap or S-trap as appropriate), all sealing washers checked, hand-tightened then snugged.
   - *Taps:* Full tap replaced if internals are no longer available or if the tap body is corroded/split.
6. **Reassembly & Test** — Water turned back on slowly. Fitting tested under normal operating pressure. Engineer checks every joint for drips over 2–3 minutes. Wipes dry and re-checks.
7. **Clean-Up & Advise** — Work area cleaned. Customer shown the fault and the fix. Advised on any age-related wear that may need attention in future.

**What a Resident Can Safely Do:**
- Identify where the leak is visible
- Send a photo or short video
- Isolate water at a local valve or stop tap if they know how
- Avoid using the faulty fitting
- Place a towel or bucket to catch drips
- Clean a tap aerator (unscrew spout tip, rinse mesh, refit)
- Do a very minor visible check (e.g., "is the drip from the spout or the base?")

**What a Resident Should Not Do:**
- Remove fittings they're not confident with
- Keep tightening random joints hoping to stop a leak
- Open walls, floors, or ceilings
- Attempt to repair hidden leaks
- Do anything involving gas equipment
- Carry out major alterations

**Triage Questions:**
- What exactly is leaking or not working?
- Is the leak from the fitting itself, the base, or from below?
- Is the leak constant or only when in use?
- Can you send a photo?
- Have you managed to turn the water off locally?
- Is it just one fitting or more than one?

**When to Guide:**
Guide only if the issue is visible, non-gas, no major damage risk, customer feels comfortable, and only a small basic check is needed.

**When to Book:**
- Customer doesn't want to do the check
- Leak is ongoing
- Isolation is unclear
- Fitting needs opening/replacing
- Problem is recurring
- There is water damage risk

### 7.2 TAPS, TOILETS, BASINS, SINKS & WASTES

**Scope:**
Dripping taps, taps not turning properly, mixer tap faults, loose taps, leaking basin wastes, leaking sink traps, toilet not flushing, toilet continuously running, toilet fill problems, slow-draining basins, waste pipe smells, overflow issues.

**How a Professional Plumber Fixes These (On-Site Process):**

*Taps:*
1. Isolate hot and cold supplies via service valves beneath the basin/sink.
2. Remove tap handle and headgear (traditional) or cartridge (modern mixer).
3. Inspect washer, seat, cartridge, or ceramic disc for wear.
4. Replace failed component — washer, cartridge, or full tap if body is corroded.
5. Reassemble, restore water, and test under pressure for 2–3 minutes.

*Toilets:*
1. Lift cistern lid and observe the flush/fill cycle.
2. **Continuously running toilet:** Usually a failed flush valve seal (diaphragm washer or drop valve seal) or a faulty fill valve that won't shut off. Engineer replaces the specific failed component.
3. **Not flushing properly:** Could be a broken syphon diaphragm (older toilets), a jammed drop valve (modern), or insufficient water level. Engineer replaces syphon, drop valve, or adjusts fill height.
4. **Fill problems:** Float valve sticking, inlet blocked with debris, or diaphragm perished. Engineer strips, cleans, or replaces the fill valve assembly.
5. Test multiple flushes and confirm fill stops at correct level.

*Basin/Sink Wastes & Traps:*
1. Place bucket beneath trap.
2. Unscrew trap (hand-tight or slip-nut connections).
3. Clear blockage or inspect for cracks, failed seals, or incorrect fall.
4. Replace trap/waste if damaged. Refit with new sealing washers.
5. Run water and check for leaks at every joint.

**What a Resident Can Do:**
- Send photos/video
- Describe whether the issue is visible, intermittent, or constant
- Stop using the fitting
- Remove visible hair/debris from a basin plug area
- Use a simple plunger on a locally blocked sink
- Flush once to demonstrate the fault
- Tell whether water is leaking onto the floor or only internal

**What a Resident Should Not Do:**
- Dismantle toilet internals unless very confident
- Use aggressive chemical drain products as a first response
- Force seized fittings
- Keep using a leaking toilet that is damaging the floor
- Ignore a continuously running toilet for days

**Triage Questions:**
- Is it leaking, blocked, not flushing, or running continuously?
- Is water going onto the floor?
- Is it one toilet or all toilets?
- Is the sink slow or completely blocked?
- Does it smell?
- Is the issue under the basin/sink or above?

**When to Book:**
- Toilet running continuously
- Floor leak
- Repeated blockage
- Trap or waste leak
- Customer uncomfortable doing anything
- Issue not resolved by basic visible steps

### 7.3 SHOWERS & BATHS

**Scope:**
Shower not getting hot enough, shower leaking, shower tray/waste leak, poor shower pressure, shower valve fault, bath waste leak, bath tap issue, shower hose/head replacement, shower not draining, bath overflow issue.

**How a Professional Plumber Fixes These (On-Site Process):**

*Shower Valve/Mixer Issues:*
1. Test hot and cold supplies independently to determine if it's a supply problem (low pressure on one side, no hot water from boiler) or a valve problem.
2. Isolate water supplies. Remove valve cover/handle to access cartridge or internals.
3. Inspect thermostatic cartridge (if fitted) — lime scale, wax element failure, or seized mechanism. Clean or replace cartridge.
4. For manual mixers — check ceramic discs, washers, and spindles. Replace as needed.
5. Reassemble, restore supplies, test temperature blend and flow rate.

*Shower Tray/Waste Leaks:*
1. Run shower and observe where water escapes — around tray edge, from waste below, or from the door/screen seal.
2. If below the tray: access the trap (via removable panel or access hatch). Check waste connection, trap seals, and flexible connector.
3. Reseal or replace waste fitting, trap, or flexible connector. Apply silicone to tray-to-wall junction if failed.
4. Test by running shower for 5+ minutes and checking below for drips.

*Pressure Issues:*
1. Check whether low pressure affects hot only, cold only, or both. Compare with other outlets.
2. If hot only — likely boiler/cylinder issue, not shower. Route to hot water troubleshooting.
3. If both — check mains pressure, any inline filters, and shower hose for kinks.
4. If cold only — check stop tap is fully open, look for partially closed isolation valves.

**What a Resident Can Do:**
- Stop using the shower if it is causing a leak
- Send photo/video
- Note whether the leak only happens when the shower is on
- Clean a shower head (soak in white vinegar solution overnight)
- Replace a simple hose or shower head if confident
- Note whether pressure is low on hot only, cold only, or both

**What a Resident Should Not Do:**
- Remove concealed shower valves
- Open walls or trays
- Ignore leaks dripping into ceilings below
- Alter temperature controls on safety devices blindly

**Triage Questions:**
- Is it leaking only when the shower is used?
- Is there any water showing below the bathroom (ceiling below)?
- Is it a temperature issue, pressure issue, leak, or blockage?
- Is it just the shower or also taps elsewhere?
- Can you send a photo or short video?

**When to Book:**
- Ceiling leak
- Hidden leak suspected
- Valve issue
- Poor temperature not linked to a simple setting
- Repeated shower tray/waste issue
- Customer doesn't want to attempt anything

### 7.4 LEAKS & BURST PIPES

**Scope:**
Visible pipe leak, burst pipe, leaking joint, hidden leak, ceiling leak, wall leak, floor leak, leak under sink, leak under bath, outside pipe leak, suspected frost damage.

**How a Professional Plumber Repairs These (On-Site Process):**

*Visible Pipe Leak:*
1. Isolate water supply (local valve or mains stop tap).
2. Drain affected section of pipework.
3. Identify failure point — compression fitting, soldered joint, push-fit connector, or pipe itself (pinhole corrosion, frost split, mechanical damage).
4. Cut out damaged section using pipe cutters (never a hacksaw on live pipework without isolation).
5. Fit replacement section with appropriate jointing method (compression, push-fit, or solder depending on pipe type and location).
6. Restore water gradually. Pressure-test repaired section. Check all joints.

*Burst Pipe (Frost Damage):*
1. Confirm pipe is isolated and drained.
2. Inspect the split — copper pipes typically split longitudinally when frozen. Plastic pipes may crack or disconnect at push-fit joints.
3. Cut out split section (minimum 50mm either side of damage).
4. Fit new pipe section. Check adjacent joints — frost often damages multiple points.
5. Insulate exposed pipework in loft spaces, garages, and external walls to prevent recurrence.

*Hidden Leak (Ceiling/Wall/Floor):*
1. Assess visible evidence — staining pattern, active dripping, bubbling paint, swollen plasterboard.
2. Determine whether leak is from supply (pressurised = constant drip), waste (only when fixture used), or heating (check system pressure gauge).
3. Gain access — remove ceiling tiles, cut inspection hatches, or lift floorboards as needed.
4. Locate and repair source. Replace damaged section.
5. Advise on drying-out period and any decoration/restoration needed (not the plumber's scope but customer needs to know).

**What a Resident Can Do:**
- Turn off the local valve or main stop tap if known
- Stop using the affected fitting or area
- Move belongings away from the water
- Place towels/buckets
- Send photos/video
- Describe when the leak gets worse (all the time vs. when using a fitting)

**What a Resident Should Not Do:**
- Cut into walls, ceilings, or floors
- Keep using a leaking shower/bath when water is going through the ceiling
- Apply random sealants as a permanent repair
- Ignore leaks near electrical fittings

**Precaution Messages:**
> "Please stop using that fitting for now."
> "If you know where the stop tap is, turn the water off."
> "Keep clear of any electrics near the leak."
> "Place a bucket or towels underneath for now."

**Triage Questions:**
- Where is the water showing?
- Is the leak constant or only when something is used?
- Has the ceiling started sagging or bubbling?
- Is it hot water, cold water, or unknown?
- Is any electrical fitting nearby?
- Can you send a photo or video?

**When to Book Urgently:**
- Active ceiling leak
- Burst pipe
- Major leak
- Leak near electrics
- Customer cannot isolate water
- Damage spreading quickly

### 7.5 DRAINAGE & BLOCKAGES

**Scope:**
Blocked sink, blocked bath waste, blocked shower waste, blocked toilet, repeated drainage issue, foul smell, slow draining, backflow, outside gully issue, shared drain suspicion, soil-stack issues.

**How a Professional Plumber/Drainage Specialist Clears These (On-Site Process):**

*Local Waste Blockage (Sink/Basin/Bath):*
1. Confirm which fixtures are affected — single fixture = local blockage; multiple = branch line or main drain.
2. Place bucket beneath trap. Remove and clean trap.
3. If blockage is beyond the trap: use a drain snake, hand auger, or flexible drain rod to clear the branch pipe.
4. Flush with running water. Confirm free flow.
5. Refit trap. Check all joints for leaks.

*Blocked Toilet:*
1. Attempt clearance with a flange plunger (creates better seal on toilet outlets than a standard plunger).
2. If plunger fails: use a toilet auger / closet auger — a flexible cable designed to navigate the toilet pan's internal trap without scratching the porcelain.
3. If still blocked: the blockage is likely in the soil pipe downstream. Access the nearest rodding point or inspection chamber.
4. Rod through the blockage. Flush and test.

*Main Drain / External Drainage:*
1. Lift inspection chamber cover. Assess whether the chamber is full (blockage downstream), empty (blockage upstream), or flowing (blockage may be local to the fixture).
2. Use drain rods, high-pressure water jetting, or electro-mechanical clearing equipment depending on severity.
3. Clear blockage. Flush system. Check multiple fixtures to confirm free flow.
4. Advise on root cause — tree root intrusion, collapsed pipe section, fat/grease build-up, displaced joints, or inappropriate items flushed.
5. Recommend CCTV drain survey if structural damage suspected.

**What a Resident Can Do:**
- Say which fixtures are affected (one or several)
- Remove visible hair/debris from local waste grilles
- Use a simple plunger for a locally blocked sink/basin
- Stop using overflowing fixtures
- Send video if water is backing up

**What a Resident Should Not Do:**
- Keep pouring chemicals into recurring blockages
- Continue using a backing-up toilet
- Open dangerous external drainage points without training
- Assume every blockage is local when multiple fixtures are affected

**Triage Questions:**
- Is it one fixture or several?
- Is it slow draining or fully blocked?
- Is there a smell?
- Is anything backing up elsewhere?
- Is it internal only or also outside?
- Is the toilet involved?

**Red Flags (Book Urgently):**
- Sewage backflow
- Overflowing foul water
- Blockage affecting multiple fixtures
- Hazardous external drainage condition

### 7.6 HOT WATER SYSTEMS (VENTED / GENERAL)

**Scope:**
No hot water, intermittent hot water, hot water takes too long, cylinder not reheating, immersion-related fallback, programmer timing issues, cylinder thermostat issues, hot-only temperature problems.

**How a Professional Heating Engineer Diagnoses & Repairs (On-Site Process):**

1. **Identify the system type:** Combi (instant hot water, no cylinder) vs. system/conventional (separate hot water cylinder). This changes the diagnostic path entirely.

2. **Combi — No Hot Water:**
   - Check boiler fires on hot water demand (open a hot tap — does the boiler ignite?).
   - If boiler fires but water isn't hot: check flow rate (combi boilers lose temperature at high flow). Check DHW temperature setting. Inspect diverter valve — may be stuck in heating position.
   - If boiler doesn't fire on hot water demand: check demand signal, flow switch/sensor, PCB fault.
   - Repair or replace failed component (diverter valve, flow sensor, PCB).

3. **Cylinder System — No Hot Water:**
   - Check programmer/timer — is hot water scheduled? Many "no hot water" calls are simply timing issues.
   - Check cylinder thermostat — is it set correctly (usually 60°C)? Is it calling for heat?
   - Check motorised valve — is the hot water zone valve opening? Listen for motor operation. Check actuator.
   - Check boiler is responding to zone valve demand.
   - If boiler fires and valve opens but cylinder doesn't heat: check circulation to the cylinder coil, check for airlocks, check pump operation.
   - Replace failed valve, thermostat, or repair circulation issue.

4. **Immersion Heater (Backup):**
   - If the immersion is the only heat source (e.g., boiler broken), check it's switched on, check the immersion thermostat, check the electrical supply/fuse.
   - Replace immersion element or thermostat if failed.

**What a Resident Can Do:**
- Confirm whether they have a combi or separate cylinder
- Check timer/programmer settings
- Say whether heating works but hot water does not
- Say whether hot water is hot anywhere in the house
- Send photo of controls

**What a Resident Should Not Do:**
- Interfere with cylinder safety components
- Remove covers or controls they don't understand
- Assume every hot water issue is a boiler issue

**Triage Questions:**
- Do you have a combi or a separate hot water cylinder?
- Do you have heating as normal?
- Is there no hot water at all or just not very hot?
- Is the problem all day or at certain times?
- Can you send a photo of the controls?

**When to Book:**
- Hot water still not working after basic setting checks
- Cylinder timing seems correct but no result
- Repeated hot-water failure
- Suspected valve/thermostat/cylinder fault
- Customer not comfortable checking settings

### 7.7 UNVENTED HOT WATER CYLINDERS

**Scope:**
Hot water discharge from pipe, tundish discharge, pressure/safety-device concerns, no hot water from unvented cylinder, expansion/safety-related issues, warning-label faults.

**How a Qualified Engineer Handles These (On-Site Process):**

Unvented cylinders operate at mains pressure (typically 3–6 bar) and include multiple safety devices: temperature/pressure relief valve (T&P valve), expansion vessel or internal air gap, tundish (visible break in discharge pipework), and thermal cut-out. These must be inspected and maintained by a competent person — the engineer must hold an unvented hot water qualification (e.g., G3).

1. **Discharge from Tundish/T&P Valve:**
   - This means a safety device has operated. Do not cap or block it.
   - Check expansion vessel charge pressure (typically 3 bar when system is cold and empty). If vessel has lost charge, water expands into the safety valve territory.
   - Check T&P valve — is it passing due to a failed seal, or is it operating correctly because the system is genuinely over-temperature/pressure?
   - Check thermostat and high-limit stat — is the cylinder overheating?
   - Re-pressurise expansion vessel, replace T&P valve, or replace thermostat/high-limit stat as needed.

2. **No Hot Water from Unvented Cylinder:**
   - Check heat source (boiler zone valve, immersion heater, or both).
   - Check cylinder thermostat setting and operation.
   - Check for airlocks in the coil circuit (less common on unvented but possible).
   - Diagnose and repair as per standard cylinder procedure, with attention to the mains-pressure environment.

**What a Resident Can Do:**
- Identify whether hot water is discharging visibly (dripping or flowing from a pipe, usually outside or into a drain)
- Switch the heater off if there is a discharge fault
- Send a photo or video
- Stop further tampering
- Report whether there is hot water or not

**What a Resident Must Never Do:**
- Remove or adjust any safety component
- Interfere with discharge pipework
- Cap or block discharge pipes
- Keep using the system normally while discharge continues
- Attempt to re-pressurise or adjust the expansion vessel

**Triage Questions:**
- Is hot water coming out of a discharge pipe or tundish?
- Do you still have hot water?
- Is the discharge continuous or occasional?
- Can you send a photo/video?
- Have you switched the heater off?

**Book Urgently When:**
- Hot water discharging from any pipe
- Visible safety discharge
- No hot water on an unvented system
- Customer unsure what they're looking at but discharge is visible

**Emergency Message:**
> "Please switch the heater off and don't adjust any components. This needs urgent attention from a qualified engineer."

### 7.8 CENTRAL HEATING SYSTEMS

**Scope:**
No heating, some radiators cold, upstairs/downstairs imbalance, system pressure changes, heating not following programme, zone issues, circulation issues, pump/valve/control faults, underfloor heating routing, system warm but not distributing correctly.

**How a Professional Heating Engineer Diagnoses & Repairs (On-Site Process):**

1. **No Heating at All:**
   - Check thermostat is calling for heat (set higher than room temperature).
   - Check programmer/timer — is heating scheduled?
   - Check boiler is firing (listen for ignition, check display).
   - If boiler fires but radiators stay cold: check circulation pump — is it running? Is it seized? Check for airlock.
   - Check motorised valve(s) — is the heating zone valve opening? Listen for actuator motor. If valve is stuck closed, heating water won't circulate.
   - Replace pump, valve actuator, or clear airlock as needed.

2. **Some Radiators Cold / Imbalance:**
   - If radiator is cold at the top: air trapped. Bleed using a radiator key.
   - If radiator is cold at the bottom but warm at top: sludge (magnetite) build-up. Needs flushing — either individual radiator removal and flush, or full system powerflush.
   - If one radiator is completely cold: check TRV (thermostatic radiator valve) — pin may be stuck down. Tap gently or remove TRV head and check pin movement. Check lockshield valve is open.
   - If whole floor/zone is cold: motorised valve for that zone may have failed. Or balancing issue — system not balanced (lockshield valves set incorrectly).

3. **Repeated Pressure Loss:**
   - Check expansion vessel — if the diaphragm has failed, system pressure rises when hot and drops when cold. Re-charge or replace vessel.
   - Check pressure relief valve — is it dripping? If so, either the vessel has failed (causing over-pressure when hot) or the valve itself is passing.
   - Check all visible radiator valves, joints, and connections for slow drips.
   - If no visible leak: there may be a hidden leak under floors or in walls. Requires leak detection.

**What a Resident Can Do:**
- Check thermostat setting
- Check timer/programme
- Say whether heating and hot water are both affected or just one
- Say if only some radiators are cold
- Bleed a radiator if confident and physically able
- Send photos of controls

**What a Resident Should Not Do:**
- Open up heating controls or junction boxes
- Drain and refill the system without knowing the procedure
- Assume the boiler is broken when only one radiator is cold
- Interfere with motorised valves or pumps

**Triage Questions:**
- Do you have hot water as normal?
- Are all radiators affected or only some?
- Is the thermostat calling for heat?
- Has the boiler pressure dropped?
- Did the issue start suddenly or gradually?

**When to Book:**
- Heating still not working after simple thermostat/timer check
- Only some zones heating
- Repeated pressure drop
- Possible pump/valve/control fault
- Customer doesn't want to bleed/check anything

### 7.9 RADIATORS, VALVES, AIR & BALANCING

**Scope:**
Cold radiator, warm at bottom only, warm at top only, noisy radiator, leaking radiator, leaking TRV, stuck radiator valve, towel rail not heating.

**How a Professional Plumber/Heating Engineer Fixes These (On-Site Process):**

*Bleeding a Radiator:*
1. Turn heating off and wait for radiators to cool slightly.
2. Place a cloth beneath the bleed valve (small square valve, usually top corner).
3. Open with a radiator key — quarter turn anti-clockwise.
4. Air hisses out. When water starts to dribble, close the valve.
5. Check boiler pressure gauge — bleeding drops system pressure. Top up via filling loop if needed (usually to 1–1.5 bar cold).

*Stuck TRV:*
1. Remove the TRV head (usually unscrews or unclips).
2. The brass pin underneath should spring up and down freely. If stuck down, the radiator won't get hot.
3. Gently tap the pin with pliers or a small hammer to free it. Work it up and down.
4. If pin won't free: replace the TRV body (requires draining the radiator).
5. Refit TRV head and test.

*Leaking Radiator Valve:*
1. Determine where the leak is — from the valve spindle (gland), the compression nut connecting valve to pipe, or the valve tail connecting to the radiator.
2. *Spindle leak:* Tighten gland nut gently, or repack the gland with PTFE tape/gland packing.
3. *Compression nut:* Tighten carefully. If overtightened or olive damaged, drain down, disassemble, and re-make the joint.
4. *Valve tail:* Drain radiator, remove valve, clean threads, re-wrap with PTFE, refit and tighten.

*System Balancing:*
1. Turn all lockshield valves fully open. Turn all TRVs to maximum.
2. Turn heating on. Note the order radiators heat up.
3. Starting with the first radiator to heat: close lockshield fully, then open it gradually (usually 1/4 to 1/2 turn). Use a pipe thermometer to check the temperature difference between flow and return pipes (target: ~12°C difference).
4. Move to the next radiator. Repeat. Radiators furthest from the boiler typically need their lockshields more open.
5. This ensures even heat distribution across the system.

**What a Resident Can Do:**
- Say whether it's one radiator or many
- Describe whether the radiator is cold top/bottom/all over
- Bleed a radiator if confident and safe to do so
- Place a towel beneath a minor valve drip
- Send a photo

**What a Resident Should Not Do:**
- Force seized valves
- Remove a radiator
- Start draining the system
- Ignore an active leak

**Triage Questions:**
- Is it one radiator or all?
- Is it cold at the top, bottom, or all over?
- Is the valve leaking?
- Have you bled it before?
- Is the boiler pressure normal?

**When to Book:**
- Radiator leak
- Valve fault
- Repeated cold radiator after bleed
- Customer elderly / physically unable / uncomfortable
- Issue appears system-wide

### 7.10 BOILERS

**Scope:**
Boiler service (annual), boiler breakdown, error code, warning light, no power, no heating, no hot water, low pressure, pilot light issues (older boilers), frozen condensate pipe, repeated lockout, boiler replacement routing, boiler safety concerns.

**How a Gas Safe Engineer Diagnoses & Repairs (On-Site Process):**

*Annual Boiler Service (21-Point Inspection):*
1. Visual inspection of boiler, flue, and surrounding area.
2. Check gas supply pressure (standing and working).
3. Remove boiler casing. Clean burner and heat exchanger.
4. Inspect ignition system, flame picture, and electrode condition.
5. Check condensate trap and drain for blockages.
6. Check expansion vessel pressure (typically 0.75–1.5 bar depending on manufacturer).
7. Check system pressure and top up if needed.
8. Inspect all seals, gaskets, and electrical connections.
9. Perform combustion analysis with calibrated flue gas analyser — check CO/CO2 ratio against manufacturer specs.
10. Check flue terminal externally for blockages or damage.
11. Test all safety devices (overheat stats, pressure relief valve, etc.).
12. Reassemble, test operation on heating and hot water, issue written service record.

*Boiler Breakdown Diagnosis:*
1. Note exact error/fault code from the display. Cross-reference with manufacturer's fault code database.
2. Common fault categories:
   - **Ignition failure (e.g., F28, F75 on Vaillant; EA on Worcester):** Check gas supply, ignition electrode, spark lead, gas valve, and PCB.
   - **Overheat/high temperature (e.g., F20 on Vaillant; E9 on Worcester):** Check pump operation, circulation, blockages, heat exchanger for sludge.
   - **Low pressure (e.g., F22 on Vaillant; E1/E9 on Worcester):** Check for visible leaks, expansion vessel, PRV. Top up and monitor.
   - **Fan fault:** Check fan operation, speed sensor, wiring.
   - **Flue fault:** Check flue for blockages, check flue sensor/APS.
3. Repair or replace the failed component. Test and clear the fault code. Monitor for recurrence.

*Frozen Condensate Pipe:*
1. Locate the condensate pipe — a white or grey plastic pipe (usually 21.5mm) running from the boiler to an external drain.
2. Identify the freeze point — usually the most exposed external section, often at a bend or where it enters the drain.
3. Apply warm (not boiling) water along the frozen section. Or wrap with warm cloths. Or use a hot water bottle.
4. Once thawed, reset the boiler. Check condensate flows freely.
5. Advise insulating the pipe or rerouting internally if freezing is recurrent.

**What a Resident Can Safely Do:**
- Check if there is power (boiler display lit? wall switch on?)
- Check breaker in the consumer unit — switch on once if tripped
- Check thermostat is set above room temperature and timer is on
- Note the exact error code on the display
- Send a photo of the display
- Check the pressure gauge (should be 1–1.5 bar when cold)
- Repressurise via the filling loop only if the manufacturer's manual instructs and the customer knows how (fill to 1–1.5 bar with heating off and boiler cool)
- Gently thaw an accessible frozen condensate pipe using warm water (never boiling)
- Follow the manufacturer's guide for any user-level reset

**What a Resident Should Not Do:**
- Open the boiler casing
- Repair any internal boiler component
- Touch gas-side components (gas valve, burner, gas pipe)
- Keep resetting the boiler endlessly after repeated lockouts
- Continue using a boiler they suspect is unsafe
- Try to fix a boiler in a rented property (notify the landlord instead)

**Boiler Triage Questions:**
- Do you smell gas? (If yes → emergency route immediately)
- Is the carbon monoxide alarm going off? (If yes → emergency route)
- What is the exact error code?
- Is the screen blank or lit?
- Do you have no heating, no hot water, or both?
- Is the pressure low (below 1 bar)?
- Have you checked the power switch and breaker?
- Do you have a combi or a separate cylinder?
- Can you send a photo of the display?

**Boiler Severity Classification:**

| Severity | Scenario |
|----------|----------|
| Green | Power/settings issue, low pressure, frozen condensate, simple user setting |
| Amber | Error remains after basic checks, repeated pressure drop, no heating/HW unresolved, repeated lockout |
| Red | Gas smell, CO alarm, severe safety concern |

**Boiler Emergency Response:**
> "Please don't do anything further with the boiler. Turn it off, open windows, go outside, and call the National Gas Emergency Service on 0800 111 999."

### 7.11 GAS SAFETY & LANDLORD CP12 CERTIFICATES

**Scope:**
Landlord gas safety check (CP12), annual gas safety record, gas appliance safety, flue safety, tenant/landlord responsibility routing, letting-agent coordination.

**How a Gas Safe Engineer Performs a Gas Safety Check (On-Site Process):**

1. **Identify all gas appliances** in the property — boiler, gas fire, gas hob, gas oven, gas water heater.
2. **For each appliance:**
   - Check gas tightness on supply pipework.
   - Test standing and working gas pressure.
   - Check burner pressure and gas rate.
   - Perform combustion analysis (flue gas readings — CO, CO2).
   - Check flue flow and spillage (ensure combustion products are safely removed from the property).
   - Check ventilation is adequate for the appliance.
   - Check safety devices are operational.
   - Visually inspect the appliance for damage, corrosion, or unsafe modifications.
3. **Assess the overall gas installation** — check emergency control valve (ECV) accessibility, inspect visible gas pipework, check meter condition.
4. **Issue the Gas Safety Record (CP12):**
   - Lists every gas appliance inspected.
   - Records test results (gas pressure, CO/CO2 readings, flue flow).
   - States whether each appliance is safe to use or has defects.
   - Signed by the engineer with their Gas Safe registration number.
   - Dated. Valid for 12 months.
5. **Hand over / distribute:**
   - Landlord must keep the record for 2 years.
   - Existing tenants must receive a copy within 28 days.
   - New tenants must receive a copy before move-in.

**Legal Context:**
- Gas Safety (Installation and Use) Regulations 1998.
- Responsibilities sit with the landlord. They cannot be transferred to the tenant.
- For HMOs (Houses in Multiple Occupation), additional requirements may apply under local authority licensing conditions.

**What the Bot Should Do:**
- Confirm whether the enquirer is a landlord, letting agent, or tenant.
- If landlord/agent: proceed to book the CP12 check. Collect property details and preferred slot.
- If tenant reporting a fault: advise them to contact their landlord or managing agent first. Offer to help structure the fault details. If the company also works directly with landlords/agents, offer to coordinate.

**Triage Questions:**
- Is this for a landlord, letting agent, or tenant?
- Is the property occupied?
- Is this for an annual gas safety check or a fault?
- Do you need the check booked at a specific property?
- What is the address and preferred slot?

**What the Bot Must Not Do:**
- Give legal advice beyond the core official duties
- Suggest tenants can take over the landlord's legal responsibility
- Suggest any non-Gas-Safe person can do gas work

### 7.12 BATHROOM & KITCHEN PLUMBING INSTALLATIONS

**Scope:**
New bathroom plumbing, bathroom refit, kitchen sink and tap plumbing, appliance water connection (washing machine, dishwasher), waste relocation, pipe reroutes, sanitaryware installation, bath replacement, shower installation plumbing, hot and cold feed alterations.

**How a Professional Plumber Handles Installations (On-Site Process):**

*Bathroom Refit (Plumbing Scope):*
1. **Survey** — Assess existing pipework layout, waste positions, supply routes. Determine what can be reused vs. what needs rerouting.
2. **First Fix** — Strip out old sanitaryware. Reroute hot and cold supply pipes to new positions. Reroute waste pipes. Fit new waste connections through walls/floors.
3. **Pressure Test** — Test all new supply pipework under pressure before tiling/boarding covers the pipes.
4. **Second Fix** — After tiling is complete, fit and connect sanitaryware: basin, toilet, bath, shower valve, taps, wastes, traps, overflows.
5. **Commission** — Test every fixture. Check flow rates, waste drainage, trap seals, and joints. Check for leaks.

*Kitchen Plumbing:*
1. Disconnect existing sink/taps. Remove waste.
2. Fit new sink unit. Connect hot and cold supplies (usually flexible tap connectors to isolation valves).
3. Fit waste kit — basket strainer, trap, waste pipe to stack or external waste.
4. Connect dishwasher/washing machine if needed — supply via self-cutting valve or dedicated isolation valve, waste via standpipe or spigot on trap.
5. Test all connections.

**Notification Requirements:**
WaterSafe says certain planned work must be notified to the water supplier before starting:
- A bath over 230 litres capacity
- A bidet with upward spray or flexible hose
- Pumps or boosters over 12 litres per minute drawing from the mains
- Some garden irrigation systems
- Large pools or ponds

**Triage Questions:**
- Is this a repair or a new installation?
- What exactly are you changing?
- Is it a bathroom, kitchen, or utility area?
- Are you moving pipework or just replacing existing items in the same positions?
- Can you send photos of the current setup?

**When to Book Survey/Visit:**
- Any planned alteration (not just a like-for-like swap)
- Bathroom refit
- Kitchen plumbing change
- Shower/bath replacement
- Major reroute
- Uncertain scope

### 7.13 OUTSIDE TAPS, EXTERNAL PLUMBING & WATER SUPPLY

**Scope:**
Outside tap repair/replacement, external pipe leak, supply pipe concern, outside stop tap routing, irrigation queries, external water-feed issues.

**How a Professional Plumber Handles These (On-Site Process):**

*Outside Tap Replacement:*
1. Isolate internal supply valve feeding the outside tap.
2. Unscrew old tap from wall plate. Check wall plate and pipework condition.
3. Fit new outside tap — bib tap with check valve (double-check valve to prevent backflow, required under Water Regulations).
4. Restore water and test. Check for leaks at wall plate and all joints.
5. If no existing check valve: fit one to comply with water regulations.

*External Pipe Leak:*
1. Determine whether the pipe is the customer's responsibility (usually from the property boundary to the house) or the water company's (usually from the main to the boundary/external stop tap).
2. If customer's responsibility: locate the leak by tracing the pipe route. Excavate if below ground (or arrange mini-excavation).
3. Repair or replace the damaged section — typically MDPE (blue plastic) for underground supply pipes.
4. Pressure-test and backfill.
5. If water company's responsibility: advise the customer to contact their water supplier (Anglian Water in the Peterborough area).

**What a Resident Can Do:**
- Send a photo
- Describe whether the leak is at the tap or below ground
- Isolate water if safe and known (internal stop tap)
- Avoid using the outside tap

**What a Resident Should Not Do:**
- Dig blindly
- Assume underground leaks are simple DIY repairs
- Modify external water systems without proper advice

**Triage Questions:**
- Is the leak at the tap itself or elsewhere?
- Is the problem above ground or below ground?
- Is it a simple replacement or a bigger issue?
- Can you send a photo?

### 7.14 ELECTRICAL CROSSOVER / CONTROLS

**Scope:**
Boiler breaker tripping, controls not powering, programmer/display dead, heating-control issue that may be electrical, electric shower routing, any plumbing/heating issue where electrics overlap.

**How This Is Handled:**

The bot must identify when the issue may not be pure plumbing and route correctly:

1. **Boiler breaker tripping:** If the breaker trips once, the customer can try resetting it once. If it trips again immediately, this indicates an electrical fault (short circuit, earth fault on the boiler or its wiring). Do not advise further resets. The boiler needs inspection by a Gas Safe engineer who can also assess the electrical components, or a qualified electrician if the fault is in the house wiring rather than the boiler.

2. **Dead programmer/controls:** Could be a failed transformer, blown fuse in the wiring centre, or a failed programmer unit. This is within a heating engineer's scope if it's part of the heating system wiring.

3. **Electric shower:** These are mains-voltage appliances hardwired to their own circuit. Faults should be assessed by a qualified electrician unless the company has a suitably qualified engineer. The bot should not give electrical repair advice.

**Approved Document P** governs electrical safety in dwellings. Work on fixed electrical installations (new circuits, consumer unit changes, bathroom electrics) must comply with Part P and may need Building Regulations notification.

**What a Resident Can Do:**
- Check whether the breaker is off
- Switch it on once
- Report whether it trips again
- Send a photo of controls

**What a Resident Should Not Do:**
- Keep resetting a repeatedly tripping circuit
- Open electrical control panels
- Dismantle boiler electrics
